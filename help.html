🎣 What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of social engineering cyberattack where attackers disguise themselves as trustworthy entities to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information such as passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.

Phishing is one of the most common and effective cyber threats, responsible for billions of dollars in losses worldwide every year. Attacks can come through emails, text messages, websites, or even phone calls.

Common Types of Phishing

  • Email Phishing — Mass emails impersonating legitimate organizations.
  • Spear Phishing — Targeted attacks against specific individuals or companies.
  • Whaling — Phishing attacks targeting high-profile executives.
  • Smishing — Phishing via SMS text messages.
  • Vishing — Phishing through voice calls.
  • Clone Phishing — Duplicating legitimate emails with malicious links.

⚙️ How Phishing Works

Phishing attacks typically follow a structured pattern:

  1. Bait Creation — The attacker crafts a convincing message or website that mimics a trusted entity (bank, social media, company).
  2. Distribution — The phishing bait is sent to potential victims through email, SMS, social media, or fake websites.
  3. Deception — The victim is tricked into clicking a link, opening an attachment, or providing personal information.
  4. Data Harvesting — The attacker collects the victim's credentials, financial data, or personal information.
  5. Exploitation — Stolen data is used for identity theft, unauthorized access, financial fraud, or sold on the dark web.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Urgent or threatening language ("Your account will be closed!")
  • Requests for personal or financial information
  • Suspicious or misspelled sender addresses
  • Generic greetings ("Dear Customer" instead of your name)
  • Unexpected attachments or links
  • Too-good-to-be-true offers ("You've won $1,000,000!")
  • Mismatched URLs (hover to check before clicking)

🔒 Safety Tips

  • Verify the sender — Always check the email address or source carefully before responding.
  • Don't click suspicious links — Hover over links to preview the URL before clicking. If in doubt, navigate to the website directly.
  • Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) — Add an extra layer of protection to your accounts.
  • Keep software updated — Install the latest security patches for your operating system, browser, and applications.
  • Use strong, unique passwords — Use a password manager to generate and store complex passwords.
  • Be skeptical of urgency — Legitimate companies rarely demand immediate action via email.
  • Report suspicious emails — Forward phishing emails to your IT department or email provider.
  • Educate yourself and others — Stay informed about the latest phishing techniques and share knowledge with colleagues and family.
  • Use security tools — Use tools like PhishGuard to quickly check suspicious URLs and emails.
